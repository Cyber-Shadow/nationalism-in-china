Nationalism Topic: China

Reasoning for Nationalism (Gordon)

The Nationalist fervour emerged during the 20th century because of the decline of the Qing Dynasty. Living conditions in that region decreased. During the Ming Dynasty, it was one of the richest in the world. But towards the end of the Qing Dynasty, they did nothing the modernise the region. The Emperor towards the end of the Qing, Guangxu, was corrupt. As a result, the Qing Dynasty was falling over.

One of the main factors was the wars the Qing fought in. Hong Kong was taken over by the British, which resulted in more corruption, and poverty increased. Because of that, an organisation, called the God Worship Society, tried to launch a war against the Qing, to overthrow them, but due to power struggles, they failed, and the Qing remained in power. But that didn’t mean complete security for the Qing.

A war between the Qing Dynasty and Japan in 1895 resulted in a crushing defeat for China, who had to relinquish Taiwan Island and some parts of Manchuria (which although remained a part of China but had massive Japanese influence). Even worse, there was another battle, called the Boxer Rebellion. With more foreign missionaries in China, the “Boxers” did anti-foreigner clothing. At first, they fought with the Qing, but when the Qing realised that they were anti-Western, they allied with them. As a result, an Eight-Nation alliance that had USA, Austria, Germany, France, Russia, Italy, UK and Japan formed an army together, and went to war with the Qing. The allies stormed Beijing, and overwhelmed the Qing.

In every war the Qing was involved in, it not only caused great damage to the country, but it also exposed the country’s power and resulted in more political unrest. People were unhappy, and the Qing decided to come up with a new plan, called “The Reform Movement of 1898”. This was the direct result of the sino-japanese war. The reforms included introduction to Western science, technology, and culture. Of course, these rules alarmed some conservatives, which resulted in a coup between the conservatives and reformers. Eventually, the reform was cancelled, leaving the country in greater chaos.

Reasoning for Nationalism (cont.)

These events meant that the Qing Dynasty was a weakened country and was highly unprotected. Beijing was about to edge closer to another war, and It became obvious that they would not win against a major power. Because of that, the people wanted change. In 1905, Sun Yat-sen, family of a farmer, founded a new group, and named it tongmenghui (Revolutionary League). He first formed it by getting support with the Japanese, and used his revolutionary ideas, which were the “Principle of Nationalism, Principle of Democracy and Principle of People’s Livelihood” (which will be explained later). At the time, he was still overseas. In 1908, the Qing Dynasty worsened, with Emperor Guangxu’s death, which meant that Pu Yi took over at only the age of 2. He and his father had no political skills, and did not know how to guide the country. As a result, he was unable to lead the country, and the Qing was in further turmoil.

The Xinhai Revolution was a direct result of the corruption within the Qing Dynasty. Sun Yat-Sen wanted a new country under a Chinese republic style. However, the revolution started in a very unusual way. In 1911, an agreement was signed to build a rail link in Sichuan, central china. When the Qing Dynasty took over of the railway construction ownership, they paid a small sum for the construction, but that was nowhere near the constructor’s demands, so that resulted in a protest by the general public. Although this was the start of the war between the government and the people, historians think that the real beginning of the revolution was when war broke out in Wuchang, in central China. This time, the Tongmenghui, Sun Yat Sen’s group, got involved, fighting with the Qing. Although the Tongmenghui was outnumbered, they got an impressive victory, although Sun Yat Sen was overseas and not available. The victory in Wuchang led to the outbreak of the fighting nationwide. At the end of 1911, 14 out of the 23 provinces in China have their full support towards the Tongmenghui. Eventually, in 1912, Emperor Puyi, at only the age of 6, had to abdicate from his throne. Negotiations resulted between Qing delegates and the Tongmenghui, which included Sun-Yat Sen, who was back, and the Qing agreed to relinquish its power, and hand it over to sun Yat Sen, Formally forming the Republic of China.

Roles of Figures (Ricky)

Sun Yat-Sen

Sun Yat Sen was the leader of the Chinese Nationalist Party (Kuomintang) and is known as the father of modern China. He also created the chinese ideology called the “Three Principles of the People”, outlining the principles of nationalism, democracy and socialism. He believed that the only way for China to move forward in the early 1900s was to become a republic and adopt the western ways. It was developed as part of Sun Yat-Sen's philosophy to make China a free, prosperous and powerful nation. After his failure to establish a democracy in China following the 1911 republican revolution, he created the Nationalist Party (also known as kuomintang). Although he died in 1925, the Nationalist movement he helped start was starting to gain momentum in mainland China. After the Communist-Nationalist split in 1927 both the communist leader, Mao Zedong and the succeeding nationalist leader, Chiang Kai-shek claimed to be continuing the true spirit of the ‘Three Principles of the People’

Three Principles of the People

The three principles of the people was an political ideology created by Sun Yat-Sen. He used these principles as a set of beliefs to guide the newly formed Kuomintang forward. After the communists and the nationalists formed an alliance in 1922, Sun was pressured by the communists for more demands of a more formal party ideology. Subsequently, he gave a series of lectures throughout the country in which he sharpened and defined his three principles.

The first principle, nationalism, had earlier been meant opposition to the Qing Dynasty and foreign imperialism. Later, Sun had defined “nationalism” as self determination for the Chinese people, as well as the minority groups living in China at that point in time. Sun described it as reviving China’s lost nationalism and using the strength of the country’s population to fight against injustice.

The second principle, “rights of the people” often translated as “democracy”, was explained by Sun as allowing the people of China to control their own government. This could be achieved through ideas such as elections and referendums.

The final principle, “people’s livelihood” often translated as “socialism”, was the most vague of the three principles. However, historians agree that Sun seemed to have an idea of an equal amount of land ownership through a fair system of taxes.

Roles of Figures (cont.)

Kuomintang

The Nationalist Party (Kuomintang, literally translated as "National People’s Party") that governed mainland China from 1928 to 1949 and later Taiwan under Chiang Kai-shek after the nationalist’s retreat from mainland China. The KMT was focused mainly on strengthening Chinese identity, while disposing of old traditions for the sake of modernising in order to unify China under a republican government.

The party was formed in Beijing in late August 1912 with the amalgamation of six nationalist groups, intended to participate as a party in the newly formed National Assembly. ith the Chinese government being dissolved by a coup in 1913. This lead the leader, Sun Yat-Sen, to lead the party with more authoritarian force. The nationalist party owed many of its earlier successes to to the Soviet’s aid and advice as well as collaborating with the Chinese Communists.

Chiang Kai-Shek (Jiang Jieshi)

Chiang Kai-Shek was a Chinese military and political leader who lead the Kuomintang for five decades and was head of state of the Chinese Nationalist Government from 1928 to 1949. He was the successor to Sun Yat-Sen who, along with Mao Zedong, played a fundamental role in China’s history in the twentieth century. His first task after joining the Nationalists, backing Sun Yat-Sen was to create an army. He was an enthusiastic patriot, aiming to unite his divided country as well as stabilising and modernising it. Although he failed in a number of ways, he left behind a prosperous economy, allowing China to grow into an economic powerhouse. Fearing that China would become a country ruled by foreign power, Chiang helped establish the Kuomintang, which he aimed at preparing a modern army to unite China and overthrow the warlords.

Oppositions to Regime (Hanyuan)

Qing Dynasty

The Qing Dynasty (1644 - 1912) was the ruling society of China before the Kuomintang (KMT) came to power. Because of their dynastic nature, one person had all the power, and that power was selective - passed down from generation to generation.

The KMT, or at least, its predecessor, the Tongmenghui (Chinese Revolutionary Alliance, TMH), was formed in the later years of Qing rule, in 1904. It was an underground resistance movement, and was the fusion of many small revolutionary groups – the leader of this alliance was Sun Yat-sen, who later was the leader of the KMT.

The TMH was formed due to the members’ displeasure with the imperial rule of the Dynasty, and wanted to reform China into a modern nation. While initially the TMH wasn’t popular, in 1911, just one year before the Qing Dynasty’s end, the Wuchang Uprising arose.

The conflict was created because the Qing government wanted to nationalise the railway system of China to pay off debts created during the Boxer Rebellion (European countries vs. Qing China, 1899-1901). The people were extremely unhappy with this decision, and decided to rebel, forming two conflicting sides – the Qing, and the TMH. This would eventually lead up to the Xinhai Revolution.

The Xinhai revolution marked the real beginning of the KMT, as a response to the declining state of the Qing dynasty. This was kind of a follow-up to the Wuchang Uprising, and ultimately resulted in multiple rebellions from all over China. The end result is the abdication of the “Final Emperor”, Puyi, in 1912.

After the fall of the Dynasty, there were still some supporters of the Qing or dynastic rule that opposed the Kuomintang party - however, they were usually small in size and number, didn’t have much influence, and were dispatched shortly. One notable example was the Empire of China, created by Yuan Shikai (a founding member of the Republic of China) in 1915. It was opposed by the revolutionaries and the military alike, and resulted in mass rebellions before it was quashed in 1916.

Oppositions to regime (cont.)

Communist Party

The Communist Party of China (CPC) was founded in 1921, after various socialist and communist ideologies such as Marxism and Leninism started to flourish in the minds of various scholars at the time. The party developed alongside he KMT without any conflict, under the rule of the then leader of the KMT, Sun Yat-sen.

After Sun died, in March 1925, the role of leader was handed ver to Chiang Kai-shek, who was rightist and was critical towards the CPC. He attempted to marginalise the CPC’s power ver the years, which culminated in the Wuhan government expelling all communist members from the KMT. This, therefore, led to the start of the Chinese Civil War.

The Chinese Civil War is often split into two parts: pre-Sino-Japanese War and post-war. Before the Second Sino-Japanese War, the CPC created the Red Army, which consisted of mutinous National Revolutionary Army (NRA) soldiers from the KMT, and armed peasants. The forces of Chiang and the CPC struggled in what was known as the Ten-Year Conflict, before Japanese forces invade China through Nanjing.

After 1945 (retreat of Japanese forces), tensions between the two parties began to rise again. During the war between China and Japan, the CPC had gained extra power – it now had 1.2 million militia, one quarter of the Chinese landmass and one third of China’s population under its control. Furthermore, the Soviet Union transported all the conquered weapons from the Japanese to the Red Army, which therefore tipped the scales further in their favour. The US also intervened, sending around 150,000 troops to various areas.

In 1949, the conflict ended with the CPC winning, and erecting their leader, Mao Zedong, as the leader of the People’s Republic of China. This therefore caused the KMT to retreat to present-day Taiwan.

Impact of Nationalism (Craig)

The earliest form of nationalism (proto-nationalism) in China took the form of the Boxer Rebellion in which the Chinese banded together in order to protect their country from imperialist takeover/expansion and Christian missionary which naturally came with the imperialist movements. While the Chinese were successfully suppressed due to their underwhelming power, good came with the movement too, as China’s relationship with the United States improved. With the loss of the Boxer Rebellion, the last Chinese dynasty ended and an open door policy was initiated which lead to the Chinese Republic.

Early Chinese nationalism resulted in an open country, increased tolerance of foreigners and loosened tension between countries. Furthermore, the Boxer Protocol was formed to keep the peace between China and the Eight-Nation Alliance, soon after this, the last dynasty ended. Nationalism in China had essentially formed the republic of China, disallowing any countries from colonising or imperialising China. Another positive outcome was the U.S funded the Boxer Rebellion Indemnity Scholarship Program which allowed for Chinese students to study in the U.S, this spread the Chinese population into the U.S and pushed forward Chinese modernisation.

According to historians, one of the earliest forms of nationalism was ethical nationalism, formed in the twentieth century. It advocates to create a nation dominated by one race. An early example of this can be seen in China, when the Han race majority rose against the minority Manchu in the Qing dynasty in 1911. They attempted to create a state dominated by a Han majority. Since then, this form of nationalism has gave way to many other forms of nationalism around the world. To this day, ethical nationalism is still practised in Tibet and Inner Mongolia, groups that were never able to create their own ethical states.

Impact on Nationalism (cont.)

Due to the prior events of the Boxer Rebellion, China was on the of the 27 allied forces in World War I. Their motivation for joining the war was to strengthen their weak status and influence on the world through the obtention of an invitation to the post-war Peace Conference, in which forced China to declare war on Germany. Due to China’s weak military because of the recent abolishment of the Qing dynasty, China contributed to the war by sending around 140,000 labourers and workers (called the Chinese Labour Corps) to Europe in order to repair war-torn Europe, dig trenches, repairing tanks, assembled artillery shells and transported weaponry. Despite the seemingly unremarkable contribution of “sending workers”, they were the largest and longest serving non-European labour group in the war and made a huge impact on the war.

Leading up to World War II (starting in 1939), China and Japan fought the Second Sino-Chinese War . Japan’s imperialist policy lead them to invade China after the tension between the two countries was at its limit partly due to the Marco Polo Bridge Incident. During this time, Japan had won Shanghai, the Chinese capital of Nanjing (which forced China to relocate their central government) and Wuhan. China was not expected to be able to withstand the relentless attack from Japan, however, due to Kuomintang nationalist influence and their leader: Chiang Kai-shek, China simply refused to surrender to the Japanese. In their refusal to admit defeat, china proceeded to win the battles in Changsha and Guangxi and hence destroying the Japaneses means of communication to deeper parts of China. This ultimately led to a stalemate and without the nationalism introduced in China it was likely that the Chinese would have surrendered which may have lead to Japanese control of China. Furthermore, Japan could have continued to conquer the USSR, British India and other asian countries, possibly leading to world domination.

Unfortunately, both of China’s efforts in WWI and WWII were largely overlooked, due to the bad look that the Chinese communist party gave to Kuomintang. In summary, nationalism in China had major effects on the world, such as the overlooked chinese labourers in WWI, prevention of possible world domination from the Japanese in WWII. Without nationalism in China, China would be nothing like it is today with China being the manufacturing hub and one of the largest contributors of science and electronics due to the fact that Kuomintang brought modernisation into the country.. The effect of nationalism was very, very strong in China and the rest of the world.
